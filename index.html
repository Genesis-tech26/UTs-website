Issue Overview

New Clark City in Capas, Tarlac is promoted as the Philippines’ “city of the future.” It is marketed as a smart, green, world-class metropolis, designed to symbolize modern governance and national progress.

However, behind this polished image lies a deep political, social, and environmental conflict. Large areas of farmland and ancestral domains have been converted into commercial, government, and sports facilities. Long-time residents, including Aeta communities, face displacement, loss of livelihood, and erosion of cultural identity.

What appears to be progress for some has become a source of land dispossession and environmental disruption for others. This website examines tensions between national development, human rights, and environmental justice.

Background & History

New Clark City was conceptualized under the government’s Build Build Build program in 2016. The BCDA planned to transform over 9,000 hectares into a modern district with offices, sports centers, commercial hubs, and eco-friendly residential zones.

Before the project began, these lands were home to farmers and Aeta communities who relied on agriculture, hunting, gathering, and cultural traditions.

Construction cleared ancestral territories and farmlands, raising concerns about land ownership, cultural preservation, and displacement.

Impacts on Communities and Environment

Impacts on Communities

  • Farmers lost access to farmlands, affecting income and food security.
  • Relocated families were transferred to areas lacking resources, making recovery difficult.
  • Aeta communities lost hunting grounds, cultural spaces, and forest resources essential to their identity.

Impacts on the Environment

  • Forest clearing disrupted natural habitats and threatened biodiversity.
  • Changes to water pathways increased the risk of flooding and soil degradation.
  • Critics emphasize the lack of strong environmental safeguards in the planning process.

Political Debates & Stakeholders

Supporters (Government & Investors)

  • View NCC as a symbol of progress and competitiveness.
  • Argue it will increase economic activity and attract investors.
  • Claim it provides job opportunities and improves national infrastructure.

Opponents (NGOs, Farmers, Aeta Representatives, Environmentalists)

  • Criticize the lack of consultation, transparency, and fair relocation.
  • Question whether ancestral lands and community rights are being protected.
  • Raise concerns about displacement, ecological harm, and lack of accountability.

Conclusion & Recommendations

New Clark City’s development has created significant tensions between national progress and local community rights. While it symbolizes modern governance and urban growth, it has also caused displacement, cultural disruption, and environmental challenges.

  • Consult affected communities meaningfully.
  • Protect ancestral domains and cultural heritage.
  • Provide fair compensation and relocation programs.
  • Implement strict environmental safeguards.
  • Ensure marginalized groups are not pushed aside.
